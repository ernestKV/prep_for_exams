McClintock (1971) conducted a study to investigate menstrual synchrony—whether women living closely, such as in college dormitories, experience synchronization of their menstrual cycles. The study involved 135 female college students, with data collected on their menstrual cycle start dates through questionnaires. Over time, results showed that the start dates of women living together tended to align.

McClintock hypothesized that pheromones—chemical signals affecting hormonal responses—might explain this synchronization. She suggested that exposure to these pheromones could regulate menstrual cycles, reflecting a form of chemical communication. However, other factors like shared environments, diets, or social interactions might also influence synchronization.

The study, while groundbreaking, had limitations. Self-reported data and a lack of experimental controls made it hard to establish a causal link between pheromones and cycle alignment. Subsequent research produced mixed results, casting doubt on the phenomenon.

Despite these challenges, McClintock’s work laid the groundwork for studying the interaction of biological and social factors in human behavior, highlighting the subtle ways individuals may influence each other physiologically.
