Maguire (2000) investigated how cognitive processes, such as spatial navigation, contribute to neuroplasticity in the hippocampus. The study examined the brains of 16 right-handed male London taxi drivers with at least 1.5 years of experience and compared them to MRI scans of 50 right-handed males who were not taxi drivers. The aim was to determine whether navigating Londonâ€™s streets resulted in structural changes in the brain.

Using voxel-based morphometry (VBM) to measure grey matter density and pixel counting to assess hippocampal size, the study found that taxi drivers had significantly larger posterior hippocampi and smaller anterior hippocampi compared to controls. VBM also showed that the size of the right posterior hippocampus correlated with years of taxi-driving experience. No differences were found in other brain areas, suggesting that the hippocampus changes in response to environmental demands.

The study highlighted the localization of function, with the posterior hippocampus linked to the use of previously learned spatial information and the anterior hippocampus associated with encoding new spatial layouts.

As a quasi-experiment, the study could not establish causation. However, the single-blind design reduced researcher bias, as the researchers did not know whether scans belonged to taxi drivers or controls. The correlation between hippocampal size and years of driving refuted the argument that preexisting spatial abilities explained the findings. The MRI scans had high ecological validity, as participants were not required to perform tasks during scanning.

Limitations included sampling bias, as the study focused on male participants, making generalization difficult. Nonetheless, the research was ethically sound, with informed consent and no health risks from the MRI procedure.
