The study on disgust sensitivity during pregnancy investigates whether disgust, as an emotion linked to avoiding disease, is more pronounced when the immune system is most vulnerable. Disgust motivates avoidance behaviors, especially regarding food, to reduce the risk of pathogen transmission. The theory behind the study is that disgust evolved as a protective measure to guard against disease, particularly during times when an individual is more susceptible, such as during pregnancy. Pregnant women, especially in the first trimester, experience heightened vulnerability to food-borne illnesses due to the suppression of their immune response. This heightened vulnerability makes disgust sensitivity particularly important in early pregnancy to prevent harm to both the mother and fetus.

The aim of the study was to examine whether disgust sensitivity adjusts based on changes in immune system vulnerability during pregnancy. Researchers surveyed 496 pregnant women, aged 28 on average, across different trimesters to compare their levels of disgust sensitivity. Of the participants, 155 were in their first trimester, 183 in the second, and 158 in the third. The results showed that women in the first trimester exhibited greater disgust sensitivity compared to those in the second and third trimesters. These first-trimester participants also experienced more nausea, which was positively correlated with their reported disgust sensitivity.

The findings support the hypothesis that disgust sensitivity is adjusted to align with immune system changes during pregnancy, specifically compensating for the maternal and fetal vulnerability to disease in the first trimester. This increase in disgust sensitivity during the first trimester helps protect the mother and fetus from potential pathogens.

The study has some strengths, including its methodology, which separated nausea from disgust, allowing researchers to observe each independently. This distinction is important because it showed that nausea and disgust sensitivity did not always coincide, and disgust decreased as the pregnancy progressed. However, the study also has limitations, particularly its reliance on self-report methods via an online survey, which may not always produce valid responses due to factors like rushed or inattentive answering.
